# Tartu Tramline Geospatial Analysis
## Route, Technical Parameters, Passenger Demand & Cost-Effectiveness

*Based on: Tartu Üldplaneering 2040+, the 2020 feasibility study (Civitta/Artes Terrae/Stratum), ERR reporting, and comparable European projects.*

---

## 1. Context

Tartu (~97,000 residents, 2024) is Estonia's second city and home to the country's oldest university. The city lacks rail-based urban transit — all current public transport runs on buses. The dominant mobility corridors are:

- **Annelinn ↔ City Centre** (largest residential district ↔ employment/university core)
- **City Centre ↔ Maarjamõisa** (historic core ↔ Tartu University Hospital, university science campus)
- **City Centre ↔ Lõunakeskus/Ränilinn** (commercial & developing southern suburbs)
- **City Centre ↔ Ropka industrial district**
- **City Centre ↔ Raadi** (National Museum, developing neighbourhood)

A 2020 feasibility and cost-benefit study (commissioned by Tartu City, conducted by Civitta Eesti AS, AB Artes Terrae OÜ, and Stratum OÜ) confirmed positive socioeconomic returns but concluded the project is only financially viable with 70–80% EU co-financing. The Tartu Üldplaneering 2040+ (adopted October 2021) reserves tram corridor rights-of-way without formally committing to construction.

## 2. Proposed Network — Lines and Route Alignment

### 2.1 Line 1 — Core Spine (Annelinn ↔ Maarjamõisa / Lõunakeskus)

This is the primary proposed route and the only one recommended for Phase 1 construction.

**Western terminus:** Mõisavahe / Anne tänav (Annelinn)
**Eastern terminus:** Lõunakeskus (with possible extension to Ränilinn)

**Street-by-street alignment:**

| Segment | Streets / Locations | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Mõisavahe → Anne tänav | Annelinn residential core; dedicated ROW through linear greenway / Anne canal corridor |
| 2 | Anne tänav → Paju tänav | Approach to Emajõgi crossing |
| 3 | Paju → Atlantis / former Holmi bridge site | River crossing via **new Holmi bridge** (or alternative: bridge adjacent to existing Turu footbridge) |
| 4 | Vanemuise tänav (ascending) | From river north bank, uphill through cultural district — car traffic removed from this street |
| 5 | Under railway overpass | Grade separation at Lembitu/Vanemuise; existing road underpass |
| 6 | Lembitu tänav | Through Maarjamõisa district; car traffic removed |
| 7 | Nooruse tänav / Maarjamõisa campus | Tartu University Hospital, university science campus — major destination |
| 8 | Towards Lõunakeskus (Ringtee / Roosi) | Southern approach to mall and Ränilinn residential area |
| 9 | **Lõunakeskus terminus** | Tartu's largest shopping centre; park-and-ride opportunity |

**Route characteristics:**
- Total route length: ~8.97 km (incl. turnaround tracks); **~7.53 km double-track**
- Alternative (ending at Lõunakeskus without Ränilinn branch): ~7.5 km, ~€0.7 km shorter double track
- Estimated stops: ~16–18 stops at ~450–550 m average spacing
- Key river crossing: **new dedicated tram bridge at former Holmi bridge site** (near Atlantis shopping centre); ~150–200 m span over Emajõgi

**Coordinates of key nodes (WGS84 approximate):**

| Node | Lat | Lon |
|---|---|---|
| Annelinn (Mõisavahe terminus) | 58.371 | 26.673 |
| Anne tänav midpoint | 58.373 | 26.685 |
| Holmi bridge crossing (Emajõgi) | 58.379 | 26.713 |
| Vanemuise / railway underpass | 58.383 | 26.716 |
| Maarjamõisa hospital (Nooruse) | 58.385 | 26.706 |
| Lõunakeskus terminus | 58.363 | 26.718 |

### 2.2 Line 2 — Ropka Branch (Vanemuise ↔ Ropka)

**Alignment:** Vanemuise tänav → Akadeemia tänav → Riia tänav → Tähe tänav → Ropka industrial park terminus

- Length: ~3.5–4 km (from Vanemuise junction)
- Serves: Tartu city centre, Riia-Tähe commercial strip, Ropka industrial district
- **Recommendation: Defer to Phase 3 or replace with BRT.** Passenger density along the Riia/Tähe corridor does not justify tram over articulated bus at current demand levels.

---

### 2.3 Line 3 — Raadi Branch (City Centre ↔ Raadi)

**Alignment:** City centre → Narva maantee → Raadi (Estonian National Museum / Raadi Manor)

- Length: ~3–4 km
- Serves: East Tartu residential zones, Estonian National Museum (major tourist destination)
- **Recommendation: Defer to Phase 3.** Lower-density corridor; tourism benefit seasonal. Consider heritage tram concept if built.

## 3. Phased Implementation Plan

### Phase 1 — Core Spine (recommended by 2030)

**Scope:** Line 1 full alignment, Annelinn ↔ Lõunakeskus, ~9 km, incl. depot

**Why this corridor first:**
1. **Annelinn** is Tartu's largest residential district — ~27,755 residents (27% of city population) in 5.4 km²; high-density Soviet-era apartment blocks ideal for transit
2. **Maarjamõisa** campus hosts Tartu University Hospital (largest employer in South Estonia, ~7,000 staff) plus TÜ science campus
3. **Lõunakeskus** is the highest retail traffic generator in Tartu; southern anchor
4. **500 m service catchment** along Line 1 covers ~45–50% of Tartu's entire population
5. The Anne tänav greenway/canal corridor offers a **car-free right-of-way** requiring no demolition — lowest disruption of any option

### Phase 2 — Network Consolidation (2030–2035)

- Extend Line 1 to **Ränilinn** (+1.5 km) as residential development matures
- Assess ridership on core Line 1 before committing

### Phase 3 — Full Network (2035–2040)

- Line 2: Ropka branch if industrial/commercial corridor densifies
- Line 3: Raadi branch tied to National Museum visitor volumes and Raadi residential growth
- Possible new cross-city line serving Ülejõe and Tähtvere

## 4. Key Infrastructure

### 4.1 Holmi River Crossing

The single most critical and costly infrastructure element. Options:

| Option | Description | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A — New Holmi Bridge** (recommended) | Dedicated tram bridge at former Holmi bridge location (~200 m span) | Direct alignment; serves Atlantis district; best geometry | New structure ~€15–25M |
| B — Modified Turu Bridge | Tram lanes added/alongside existing Turu pedestrian bridge | Cheaper if feasible | Capacity/geometry constraints; likely full rebuild |
| C — Vabaduse puiestee routing | Route via existing road bridges | No new bridge | Significant detour; lower ridership; Vanemuise alignment lost |

**Recommendation: Option A.** The Holmi alignment is geometrically superior and avoids the car-traffic conflicts of Option C. Cost is justified given the ridership it enables.

### 4.2 Depot

- Location: Southeastern Tartu, near Lõunakeskus or Ropka industrial area
- Minimum footprint for Phase 1 fleet: ~2–3 ha
- Estimated cost: €10–15 million (included in overall Phase 1 estimate)

### 4.3 Traffic Management

Segments requiring car-traffic removal or restriction:
- **Anne tänav** (Annelinn): existing greenway amenable to tram-only or tram+bike
- **Vanemuise tänav**: car traffic removed between Emajõgi and railway; replaced by tram + pedestrian/cycle
- **Lembitu tänav**: shared-street treatment or full removal of car through-traffic
- **Nooruse tänav** (Maarjamõisa): tram + hospital access only

## 5. Technical Parameters

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **System type** | Light rail / modern tram | At-grade, dedicated ROW; not metro |
| **Track gauge** | 1435 mm (standard gauge) | European standard; not Russian broad gauge — enables competitive vehicle procurement; compatible with potential future Tallinn interconnection via Rail Baltica |
| **Electrification** | 750V DC overhead wire (OCS) | Standard for European trams; pantograph collection |
| **Vehicle type** | Low-floor articulated tram | 30–40 m length recommended (e.g., Škoda ForCity, CAF Urbos, PESA Twist class) |
| **Vehicle capacity** | 150–230 passengers/vehicle | Including standing; seated ~70–90 |
| **Fleet size (Phase 1)** | 8–12 vehicles | For 10-min headway on ~9 km line |
| **Operating speed (max)** | 50–70 km/h | On unconstrained segments |
| **Commercial speed (avg)** | 20–25 km/h | Including stops; vs. ~18 km/h for Tartu buses |
| **Headway (peak)** | 7–10 minutes | Comparable to Tampere's opening headway |
| **Headway (off-peak)** | 12–15 minutes | |
| **Stop spacing** | 400–550 m | Fewer, longer-spacing stops for speed vs. bus |
| **Platform length** | 40–45 m | For future double-unit operations |
| **Gradient (max)** | ≤60‰ | Vanemuise hill section is critical; requires review |
| **Track structure** | Embedded slab track in shared zones; ballasted in greenway sections | |
| **Signal priority** | Full TSP (tram signal priority) at all intersections | Essential for achieving commercial speed targets |

### 5.1 Vanemuise Hill Gradient

The ascent from the Emajõgi crossing to the railway underpass on Vanemuise tänav involves an elevation change of approximately 15–20 m over ~400–500 m, implying gradients of 30–50‰. Modern low-floor trams handle up to 60–80‰; this segment is **feasible but requires precise survey and vehicle specification confirmation.**

## 6. Passenger Demand Projections

### 6.1 Catchment Population

| Corridor zone | Approximate residents within 500 m |
|---|---|
| Annelinn (western section, Anne tänav) | ~22,000–25,000 |
| City centre / Ülejõe | ~8,000–12,000 |
| Vanemuise / cultural district | ~3,000–5,000 |
| Maarjamõisa campus zone | ~4,000 residents + ~7,000 employees |
| Lõunakeskus / Ränilinn | ~6,000–8,000 |
| **Total catchment (Line 1)** | **~48,000–55,000** |

This represents ~48–55% of Tartu's total population of ~97,000.

### 6.2 Ridership Estimates

Methodology: Scaling from the Tampere tram (population ~240,000; ridership ~65,000 passengers/day), adjusted for Tartu's smaller scale and lower urban density.

| Scenario | Modal assumptions | Daily boardings |
|---|---|---|
| **Conservative** | 15% of catchment population makes 1 tram trip/day | ~7,000–8,000 |
| **Base case** | 20% of catchment × 1.8 trips avg | ~17,000–20,000 |
| **Optimistic** | 25% modal shift in catchment corridor | ~24,000–28,000 |
| **Tampere-scaled benchmark** | Proportional to population ratio (97k/240k = 40%) × 65,000 | ~26,000 (upper bound) |

**Working estimate: 15,000–20,000 daily boardings** by Year 5 of operation on Line 1 alone. The 2020 Tartu study's note that the 500 m catchment covers ~50% of the population supports the base-case range.

### 6.3 Key Trip Generators

| Origin/Destination | Daily trips (est.) | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Annelinn ↔ City Centre | 8,000–10,000 | Highest |
| City Centre ↔ Maarjamõisa Hospital | 4,000–6,000 | Very high |
| Annelinn ↔ Maarjamõisa | 2,000–3,000 | High |
| All ↔ Lõunakeskus | 2,000–4,000 | High (weekend peak) |
| University corridor | 3,000–5,000 | High (academic calendar) |

## 7. Cost Estimates

### 7.1 Phase 1 Construction

| Cost component | Estimate |
|---|---|
| Track infrastructure (~9 km, incl. embedded, greenway, and bridge) | €55–65 million |
| New Holmi river bridge (dedicated tram bridge) | €15–25 million |
| Stops and passenger facilities (16–18 stops) | €5–8 million |
| Overhead catenary and power supply | €5–7 million |
| Depot (2–3 ha, basic fitout) | €10–15 million |
| Vehicles (10 trams × ~€3.5M/unit) | €33–35 million |
| Design, supervision, contingency (15%) | €18–24 million |
| **Total Phase 1 (indicative)** | **€141–179 million** |

*Note: The 2020 study cited €100 million for Phase 1 including depot and vehicles — at 2020 prices. With construction cost inflation of 30–40% since 2020, the 2025-price equivalent is approximately €130–140 million at minimum.*

### 7.2 Benchmarks (per km of double track)

| Project | Country | Year | Cost/km |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tampere Phase 1 (15 km) | Finland | 2021 | ~€16M/km (infra only) |
| Tallinn Old City Harbour (2.5 km) | Estonia | 2024 | ~€22M/km (infra only) |
| Tartu Phase 1 (2020 estimate, inflated) | Estonia | 2025 | ~€15–18M/km (incl. bridge) |
| European median (at-grade LRT) | — | 2020–24 | €15–25M/km |

Tartu's lower land values, existing greenway corridors, and simpler at-grade alignment position it at the **lower end of the European cost spectrum**, making the per-km cost competitive.

### 7.3 Funding Structure

| Source | Share | Amount (base case €150M) |
|---|---|---|
| EU Cohesion Fund / CEF | 70–75% | €105–112 million |
| Estonian state co-financing | 10–15% | €15–22 million |
| Tartu city | 10–15% | €15–22 million |

**Critical dependency:** EU structural fund access (2028–2034 funding period) is the gateway condition. Without 70%+ EU co-financing, the project is not viable for a city of Tartu's size and fiscal capacity.

### 7.4 Operating Costs and Revenue

| Item | Annual estimate |
|---|---|
| Vehicle operations & maintenance | €4–6 million/year |
| Infrastructure maintenance | €1–1.5 million/year |
| Staff (drivers, depot, admin) | €2–3 million/year |
| **Total operating cost** | **€7–10.5 million/year** |
| Fare revenue (17,500 avg daily riders × 365 × €0.80 avg yield) | ~€5.1 million/year |
| Operating subsidy required | **€2–5.5 million/year** |

For comparison, Tartu already subsidises its bus network. The tram's higher capacity-per-vehicle means it replaces multiple bus routes, partially offsetting new costs.

## 8. Cost-Effectiveness Analysis

### 8.1 Benefit-Cost Ratio

The 2020 feasibility study confirmed a **positive socioeconomic BCR** (>1.0) when including:
- Travel time savings (tram avg 20–25 km/h vs. bus ~18 km/h, with full signal priority)
- Reduced road accidents
- Emissions reductions (100% electric operation)
- Urban regeneration / land value uplift along corridor
- Reduced car infrastructure costs

**Estimated BCR: 1.2–1.6** (base case, 30-year analysis horizon, 4% discount rate), consistent with similar Nordic/Baltic projects.

### 8.2 Cost per Passenger-km

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Annual passenger-km (17,500 daily × avg 4 km trip × 365) | ~25.6 million pass-km/year |
| Annual total cost (capex annualised over 30yr @ 4% + opex) | ~€18–22 million/year |
| **Cost per passenger-km** | **€0.70–0.86** |

This is comparable to Tallinn tram and Helsinki tram operating economics.

### 8.3 Cost-Effectiveness Rankings by Segment

| Segment | Passengers/km | Infra cost/km | Cost-effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anne tänav (Annelinn greenway) | Very high (~2,000/km·day) | Low (existing greenway) | **Excellent** |
| Vanemuise tänav (bridge to railway) | High (~1,500/km·day) | Medium-high (bridge + hill) | **Good** |
| Lembitu → Maarjamõisa | Very high (~1,800/km·day) | Low-medium | **Excellent** |
| Maarjamõisa → Lõunakeskus | Medium (~1,000/km·day) | Medium | **Good** |
| Ropka branch (Line 2) | Low (~400/km·day) | Medium | **Poor** |
| Raadi branch (Line 3) | Low-medium (~500/km·day) | Medium | **Below threshold** |

## 9. Cost-Effectiveness Optimisation Recommendations

### 9.1 Priority 1 — Maximise the Core Spine

**Build Line 1 in full (Annelinn ↔ Lõunakeskus) as Phase 1.** Do not truncate it at Maarjamõisa — the Lõunakeskus extension adds only ~1.5 km but substantially increases ridership through the retail anchor effect and connects Ränilinn's growing residential base.

### 9.2 Priority 2 — Optimise the River Crossing

Select **new Holmi bridge** (Option A). While expensive individually, it:
- Provides the geometrically optimal alignment
- Enables future network expansion without rerouting
- Serves the Atlantis district and former Holmi quarter (planned mixed development)
- Is a once-in-a-generation infrastructure investment that should not be value-engineered away

### 9.3 Priority 3 — Exploit the Anne Canal Greenway

The **Anne tänav / Anne canal corridor** is Tartu's highest-value tram right-of-way because:
- It is already car-free or low-traffic
- It has space for a 7–10 m tram ROW without demolitions
- It runs through the heart of Annelinn at highest residential density
- Combined tram + cycling infrastructure creates a modal corridor benchmark

**Recommended cross-section:** 2× tram tracks (4.8 m) + 2× protected cycle lanes (3.0 m) + greenway buffer = ~10–11 m total. Fits within existing greenway width.

### 9.4 Priority 4 — Signal Priority Everywhere

Full **tram signal priority (TSP)** at all signalised intersections is non-negotiable for reaching commercial speed targets. Each 1 km/h improvement in average commercial speed reduces fleet requirements by ~1 vehicle (saving ~€3.5M in capital) and improves passenger appeal. Budget €0.5–1M for TSP hardware across the corridor.

### 9.5 Priority 5 — Defer Low-Performing Branches

| Branch | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Line 2 (Ropka) | **Defer to 2035+** | Low ridership density; articulated bus is sufficient currently |
| Line 3 (Raadi) | **Defer to 2035+** | National Museum traffic insufficient alone; reassess with Raadi district build-out |
| Ränilinn extension | **Proceed with Phase 2** | Low incremental cost; growing population base |

### 9.6 Priority 6 — Integrated Ticketing and Land Use

- **Integrate tram ticket with Tartu bus network** from Day 1 (single flat fare or free-transfer model)
- **Require transit-oriented development (TOD)** along the Vanemuise and Lembitu corridors — upzone to 4–6 storeys within 200 m of tram stops. Each additional 1,000 residents in the tram corridor generates ~300–400 additional daily tram trips
- **Negotiate park-and-ride at Lõunakeskus** — the shopping centre car park (multi-storey, ~2,000 spaces) can serve dual purpose, improving ridership without extra city investment

### 9.7 Priority 7 — EU Funding Timeline

| Milestone | Target date |
|---|---|
| Detailed design and EIA | 2025–2027 |
| EU funding application (CEF / Cohesion Fund 2028–34) | 2026–2027 |
| Construction start | 2028 |
| Operations begin (Line 1 core) | 2030–2031 |

The **2028–2034 EU funding cycle** is the window of opportunity. Missing it pushes the project to 2035+ and risks cost inflation of another 20–30%.

## 11. Summary and Key Findings

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| **Recommended Phase 1 route** | Annelinn (Mõisavahe) → Anne tänav → Holmi bridge → Vanemuise → Lembitu → Maarjamõisa → Lõunakeskus |
| **Phase 1 route length** | ~9 km (double track) |
| **Estimated stops** | 16–18 |
| **Track gauge** | 1435 mm standard gauge |
| **Phase 1 total cost (2025 prices)** | €130–180 million incl. depot and vehicles |
| **Cost per km** | ~€15–18M/km |
| **EU funding required** | 70–75% (€95–130M) |
| **City/state contribution** | €20–50M |
| **Projected daily ridership (Year 5)** | 15,000–20,000 |
| **Operating subsidy required** | €2–5.5M/year |
| **Benefit-Cost Ratio** | ~1.2–1.6 |
| **Target opening** | 2030–2031 |
| **Single most critical infrastructure** | New Holmi tram bridge over Emajõgi |
| **Single highest-value corridor** | Anne tänav greenway through Annelinn |
| **Single biggest risk** | EU funding application failure or delay |

### Bottom Line

The **Annelinn–Maarjamõisa–Lõunakeskus spine is the only segment with sufficient demand to justify tram infrastructure** in Tartu's current urban form. It is also the most cost-effective: it exploits an existing greenway corridor, serves the city's largest residential district and largest employer, and reaches the major retail anchor — all within ~9 km. EU co-financing transforms this from unaffordable to viable. Every other proposed branch (Ropka, Raadi) should be deferred until Line 1 ridership is proven.
